RED SLENDER LORIS


The red slender loris (Loris tardigradus) is a small, nocturnal strepsirrhine monkey found in Sri Lanka's rainforests. This is the sixth of the ten focus species and the 22nd of the 100 EDGE mammal species worldwide, which are the most evolutionarily different and globally endangered. L. t. tardigradus and L. t. nycticeboides have been classified as subspecies.(Animalia.bio)

PURPLE-FACED LANGUR


The purple-faced langur (Semnopithecus vetulus), sometimes known as the purple-faced leaf monkey, is an indigenous Old World primate to Sri Lanka. The animal is a long-tailed arboreal species distinguished by its predominantly brown coloring, dark face (with whiter lower face), and shy demeanor. The species was once common in suburban Colombo and "wet zone" villages (areas with high temperatures and high humidity all year, with rain deluges during the monsoon seasons), but rapid urbanization has resulted in a significant decrease in the monkey population level. It was previously classified in the lutung genus Trachypithecus, but DNA evidence indicated that it is more closely related to Semnopithecus and closely related to the gray langurs.(Animalia.bio)

SRI LANKA BLUE MAGPIE


The Sri Lanka blue magpie (Urocissa ornata) is a vividly colored member of the Corvidae family that is found only in Sri Lanka. This species has evolved to hunt in dense canopy areas, where it is highly active and nimble. Its flight, however, is quite weak, and it is rarely utilized to travel long distances. Despite the Sri Lanka blue magpie's capacity to adapt to human presence, it is classed as vulnerable to extinction due to fragmentation and degradation of its habitat of deep primary forest in southern Sri Lanka's wet zone. GOLDEN PALM CIVET


The golden palm civet (Paradoxurus zeylonensis) is a type of palm civet that is only found in Sri Lanka. The IUCN Red List classifies it as Vulnerable. Its range is highly fragmented, and the extent and quality of its habitat in Sri Lanka's hill regions are dwindling.Peter Simon Pallas described the golden palm civet in 1778.(Animalia.bio)

SRI LANKA HILL MYNA


The Sri Lanka hill myna, Ceylon myna, or Sri Lanka myna (Gracula ptilogenys) is a starling family myna. This bird is only found in Sri Lanka.This passerine is commonly found in forests and agricultural areas. The Sri Lanka myna nests in a hole. A typical clutch consists of two eggs.The plumage of these 25 cm long birds is green-glossed black with a purple tinge on the head and throat. In flight, there are enormous white wing patches that are visible. The powerful legs are brilliant yellow, and the neck has golden wattles. The Southern hill myna, which also lives in Sri Lankan forests, is distinguished by the form and position of its wattles, as well as its larger orange-red bill. The sexes are comparable.in plumage, but the iris color, which is pale in females and black in males, distinguishes them. Juveniles have a duller bill, smaller wattles, and are generally less shiny.The Sri Lanka myna, like most starlings, is omnivorous, consuming fruit, nectar, and insects.(Animalia.bio)

SRI LANKAN ELEPHANT


The Sri Lankan elephant (Elephas maximus maximus) is one of three Asian elephant subspecies. Its population is now primarily restricted to the dry zone of Sri Lanka's north, east, and southeast. Sri Lankan elephants were once fairly plentiful and extensively scattered across the tear-shaped island off India's southern coast. They are currently threatened by habitat loss and degradation, and they are unable to continue their migratory routes as a result of human development.(Animalia.bio)
